An Introduction to Two-Factor Authentication

Security is a journey, not a destination.

22 November 2015

Even if you create the strongest password in the world, there isn't much you can do to keep hackers out of your account once they get their hands on your password. People who use the same password for multiple accounts are especially screwed. If hackers discover one of your account passwords, you can bet they'll try to use it elsewhere too.

The solution to this is two-factor authentication (2FA). In addition to something you know (your password) you supply something you have, like a text message sent to your phone number or a security code generated via a key fob or an app on your phone. 2FA can also use something you are, like a fingerprint.

If you've ever visited an ATM, you've already used 2FA. Your debit card is the thing you have, and your pin is something you know. If someone steals your debit card they (usually) can't withdraw your money without your PIN. If someone figures out your PIN, there's not much they can do without your debit card.

2FA greatly improves your security. While it's easy to brute force or skim passwords for most accounts, it's much harder to gain access to the "something you have." Once an attacker compromises your password, he or she would then have to steal your phone or key fob.

Many major online services have incorporated 2FA into their accounts, but you have to opt-in to enable them. Companies like Dropbox, Google, Facebook, Twitter, GitHub, and Apple support 2FA authentication. Take some time and check to see if the services you use support 2FA and enable it where supported.

In addition, keep this in mind:

  • Never use the same password for more than one account. Use a password manager like LastPass or 1Password.
  • Create strong passwords. Using a password manager makes this even easier by providing unique randomly generated secure passwords on-demand.
